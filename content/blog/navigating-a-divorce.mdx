Divorce is one of the most significant legal and personal transitions a person can face. New York's adoption of no-fault divorce in 2010 removed the need to prove fault as grounds for dissolution — but it did nothing to simplify the financial, property, and parenting issues that must be resolved in most divorces.

This guide walks through the key issues you'll encounter in a New York divorce proceeding.

## Grounds for Divorce in New York

New York now recognises "irretrievable breakdown" of the marriage for at least six months as a no-fault ground. You simply need to state under oath that the marriage has broken down irretrievably — no proof required.

Fault-based grounds (adultery, cruel and inhuman treatment, abandonment, imprisonment) still exist and may be relevant in certain cases — particularly if fault affects equitable distribution of assets.

## Equitable Distribution of Property

New York is an equitable distribution state, meaning marital property is divided fairly — but not necessarily equally. The court considers factors including:

- Duration of the marriage
- Each spouse's income and earning potential
- Contributions to the marriage (including as a stay-at-home parent)
- Age and health of each spouse
- Any prenuptial or postnuptial agreements

**Marital vs. separate property:** Property acquired during the marriage is generally marital property subject to division. Property owned before marriage, or received as a gift or inheritance during the marriage, is generally separate — but this can become complicated when separate and marital assets are "commingled."

## Child Custody and Parenting Time

Courts determine custody based on the best interests of the child. New York recognises two types of custody:

- **Legal custody:** The right to make major decisions about the child's education, health care, and religion. Joint legal custody — where both parents share decision-making — is common.
- **Physical custody:** Where the child primarily lives. The non-custodial parent typically has parenting time (visitation) on a schedule agreed by the parties or set by the court.

Factors courts consider include each parent's relationship with the child, the ability of each parent to provide stability, any history of domestic violence, and — for older children — the child's own preferences.

## Child Support

Child support in New York is calculated using the Child Support Standards Act (CSSA) formula, which sets basic support as a percentage of the combined parental income:

- 17% for one child
- 25% for two children
- 29% for three children

This is a starting point; the court may adjust based on childcare costs, health insurance, and other factors. Support continues until the child turns 21 in New York.

## Spousal Maintenance (Alimony)

New York has guidelines for calculating spousal maintenance (formerly called alimony), considering factors like the length of the marriage, each spouse's earning capacity, and the standard of living during the marriage. Maintenance can be temporary (during the divorce proceeding) or post-divorce.
