A car accident is a jarring, disorienting experience. In the moments after impact, adrenaline is high and clear thinking is difficult. Yet what you do — and equally important, what you don't do — in the minutes, hours, and days that follow can significantly affect your ability to recover fair compensation.

## Immediately at the Scene

**1. Check for injuries and call 911.** If anyone is injured, call emergency services immediately. Even if injuries seem minor, insist on a police report — it becomes crucial documentation later.

**2. Move to safety if possible.** If the vehicles are driveable and blocking traffic, move them to the shoulder. Otherwise, turn on hazard lights and stay put.

**3. Document everything.** Use your phone to photograph:
- All vehicles from multiple angles
- License plates of all vehicles involved
- The accident scene including road conditions, traffic signs, and skid marks
- Any visible injuries (yours and others')
- The other driver's insurance card and licence

**4. Exchange information — but watch what you say.** Get the other driver's name, contact information, insurance company, and policy number. Do not admit fault, apologise, or speculate about what happened. Even saying "I'm sorry" can be used against you later.

**5. Get witness information.** If bystanders saw the accident, ask for their names and phone numbers. Witness accounts from neutral third parties carry significant weight.

## In the Hours After the Accident

**Seek medical attention — even if you feel fine.** Many serious injuries, including whiplash, concussions, and soft tissue damage, do not manifest symptoms immediately. Delayed treatment creates gaps in your medical record that insurers will use to argue your injuries aren't serious or aren't related to the accident.

**Report the accident to your insurance company.** Most policies require prompt notification. Provide the basic facts without speculating about fault or the extent of your injuries.

**Do not give a recorded statement to the other driver's insurer.** You are not required to do so, and doing so without legal counsel can harm your claim.

## In the Days and Weeks After

**Keep records of everything:** medical appointments and bills, prescription costs, missed work days, and any other out-of-pocket expenses.

**Preserve evidence.** Don't have your vehicle repaired until photos are taken and ideally an independent estimate is obtained. If you have dashcam footage, save it immediately — many cameras overwrite footage automatically.

**Consult an attorney before accepting any settlement.** Insurance companies move quickly to settle claims, often before the full extent of injuries is known. A settlement accepted too early may not cover your long-term medical costs, lost earning capacity, or pain and suffering.
